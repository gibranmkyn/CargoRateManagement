HMW: Create Order Flow

"How might we redesign the create order flow so ops can think 'what services do I need?' first -- then assign vendors -- without losing the simplicity of the 1-service-per-job model?"
Current problem: The old 3-step flow had Step 2 = "check off services" and Step 3 = "assign vendors." We simplified to 2 steps by removing the service checklist. But this lost the service-first mental model: ops thinks "I need FM, EC, CS for this shipment" BEFORE they think about which vendor does each one. Currently they have to mentally plan the jobs and add them one by one.

Repetitive friction: For a 4-job order, ops does 4x: click "Add Job" → pick service dropdown → pick vendor dropdown → pick route. The service picker is the least interesting choice (ops already knows which services are needed) but it's gating every job row creation. In a typical TikTok shipment (FM + EC + CS + OH), that's 4 rounds of the same dance.

Goal: Let ops express the service plan upfront ("this order needs FM, EC, CS"), then fill in the vendor details per job. Each job still has exactly 1 service -- we're just changing the ORDER of decisions, not the data model.

Option A: Restore the Service Checklist (3-Step Flow)

Reversal
How it works: Bring back the original 3-step form. Step 1: order details (customer, MAWB, bags, weight). Step 2: check off which services this order needs (FM, EC, CS, CR, OH). Step 3: system auto-creates one job row per checked service with the service pre-filled and locked. Ops just fills in vendor + route for each row. The key difference from the old design: each job gets exactly 1 service (pre-filled from Step 2). No multi-service jobs.
Pros
- Service-first thinking preserved -- matches ops mental model
- Less repetitive: service is pre-filled, ops only picks vendor + route
- Explicit step for "what services does this order need?"
- Easy to understand -- each step has one clear purpose
Cons
- Extra step adds friction for simple 1-job orders
- 3 steps feels heavier than 2 for power users
- Can't add duplicate services (e.g., two FM jobs for different routes) without going back
- The service checklist step is very sparse -- just 5 checkboxes

3-step form: Step 1 (order details) → Step 2 (service checklist) → Step 3 (vendor assignment)

Option B: 2-Step with Service-First Job Creation

Hybrid
How it works: Keep the 2-step form. Step 1: order details. Step 2: instead of a generic "Add Job" button, ops sees service chips (FM, EC, CS, CR, OH). Clicking a chip creates a new job row with that service pre-filled and locked. Ops then fills vendor + route in the row. Adding a job IS selecting a service -- the two actions are merged into a single click. Can click the same service again to create a second job with that service (rare, but supported).
Pros
- Only 2 steps -- no extra page for service selection
- Service-first by design: the first action per job is choosing a service
- Each click maps to exactly 1 job -- clear mental model
- Supports duplicate services (two FM jobs for different legs)
Cons
- Slightly non-standard pattern: clicking a chip creates a row
- Less explicit "plan all services first" step -- ops may still go one-by-one
- The chips and job rows coexist, which is visually busier than Option A

Option C: Quick-Add Service Bar

Recommended
How it works: Step 1: order details. Step 2: a prominent horizontal "quick-add" bar sits at the top: [FM] [EC] [CS] [CR] [OH]. Each button shows a + icon. Click any service button → a job row instantly appears below with that service locked in. The bar persists -- ops can click services in any order, click the same one multiple times (two FM legs), and see a running count on each button. Job rows accumulate below and can be filled in or removed. The bar is the command center; the rows are the result.
Pros
- Fastest flow: 1 click per job, no dropdowns for service
- All 5 services visible at all times -- ops can scan and click what's needed
- Running count shows which services are assigned (FM: 1, EC: 1, CS: 1)
- Supports duplicate services naturally (click FM twice = two FM rows)
- Service-first by design: the first and only action is clicking a service
Cons
- Similar to Option B conceptually -- the bar is just a more visual version of chips
- Ops might click too fast and create unwanted rows (easy to remove though)
- New pattern that ops needs to learn (but very intuitive once seen)

Verdict: Option C -- Quick-Add Service Bar

The quick-add bar is the fastest and most visual approach. Ops sees all 5 services at a glance, clicks the ones they need, and each click creates a pre-filled job row with the service locked. It preserves the service-first mental model (ops plans services before vendors) without adding an extra form step. The running count on each button ("FM: 1") gives instant feedback on the order's shape.

Why not Option A? The 3-step flow adds a sparse page (just 5 checkboxes) that doesn't justify its own step. Power users will find it slow. Why not Option B? It's conceptually identical to C, just less visual. The dedicated bar with counts is clearer than inline chips. Why C? It saves 4 actions on a typical 4-job order, the bar doubles as a service summary, and duplicate services are naturally supported. The pattern is immediately intuitive: click a service, get a job row.
